    Honoring the Harrison Family Legacy

    In native Louisiana, the Houma and Bayougoula Indian Nations were among the native Americans who inhabited the area along the Mississippi River. Around 1790, the area now known as North Baton Rouge and Clinton, Louisiana, would be colonized by multi-national European migrants, including French, German, English, and Spanish.

    As early as the year 1719, hundreds of enslaved men and women were taken against their will from West African nations, transported across the Atlantic Ocean, and deposited at seaports along the southeastern shores of the United States. The mostly untraceable journey of these enslaved men and women (and free black people) remains the most inhumane, brutal, and complex period in American history. Between 1719 and 1802, the number of enslaved Africans in the United States increased from 1.2 million to 4.5 million.

    The full story of Joseph Harrison's (born around 1802) and Kitty Wade Harrison's (born around 1803) birth, life, and death remains unknown. Documentation has linked the couple’s migration (perhaps from Virginia) and their permanent residency to areas in and around St. Helena Parish, including Darlington, Greensburg, Chipola, and Liverpool, and East Feliciana Parish in Felixville and Clinton, Louisiana.

    Joseph and Kitty Wade Harrison seeded nine generations to date. Over the next two hundred twenty (222) years, their descendants, armed with stamina, wit, and wisdom, have contributed to their communities' social, economic, and educational success. Across many productive careers, the Harrisons have made an impact as farmers, businesspeople, creative entrepreneurs, homemakers, educators, architects, transportation and mechanical engineers, corporate employees, leaders in the food service industries, accountants, lawyers, doctors, health care professionals, members of the military, administrators, in areas of information technology and computer engineering; all of which contributed to the building of the civil society in America.
